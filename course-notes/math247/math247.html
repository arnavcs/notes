Example Definition Results

Billinearity

Let \(V\) be a vector space over a field \(F\). A billinear form is a function \(B : V \times V \rightarrow F\) which is linear in both arguments seperately. This means that for \(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{z} \in V\) and \(a, b \in F\), we have

  1. \(\langle a\mathbf{x}+ b\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{z} \rangle = a \langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z} \rangle + b \langle \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{z} \rangle\)

  2. \(\langle \mathbf{z}, a\mathbf{x}+ b\mathbf{y} \rangle = a \langle \mathbf{z}, \mathbf{x} \rangle + b \langle \mathbf{z}, \mathbf{y} \rangle\)

Inner Product Space

For \(F\) either \(\mathbb{R}\) or \(\mathbb{C}\), we define an inner product space as a vector space \(V\) over \(F\) with the together with an inner product. This inner product is a map \(\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle : V \times V \rightarrow F\) which satisfies the following for all vectors \(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{z} \in V\) and scalars \(a, b \in F\):

  1. Conjugate Symmetry: \(\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle = \overline{\langle \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{x} \rangle}\)

  2. Linearity: \(\langle a\mathbf{x}+ b\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{z} \rangle = a \langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z} \rangle + b \langle \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{z} \rangle\)

  3. Positive-Definiteness: \(\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x} \rangle \geq 0\) with equality iff \(\mathbf{x}= \vec{0}\)

As a result we see that \(\langle \mathbf{x}, \vec{0} \rangle = \langle \vec{0}, \mathbf{x} \rangle = 0\). Also, when \(F\) is \(\mathbb{R}\) (which is what we will consider for this course), we get \(\)that conjugate symmetry reduces to symmetry, and also then that the inner product space is billinear. Thus, an inner product on a real vector space is a positive-definite symmetric billinear form.

Euclidean Vector Space

Recall from Math 146 that we define \(\mathbb{R}^n := \{ (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) : x_i \in \mathbb{R}, i = 1, 2, \ldots, n \}\) and this definition has the structure of a \(n\)-dimensional real vector space and has standard basis \(\{ e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_n \}\). Under the standard Euclidean inner product (dot product) which takes \(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \mapsto \mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{R}\) where \(\mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{y}= \sum_{i = 1}^n x_i y_i\), we see that \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is also an inner product space, which we call a Euclidean vector space.

The rest of this course mainly analyzes Euclidean spaces.

Norm

Let \(V\) be a vector space over \(F\), a subfield of \(\mathbb{R}\) (but more generally \(\mathbb{C}\)). A norm on \(V\) is a function \(\rho : V \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) which satifies the following

  1. Subadditivity: \((\forall \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \in V) (\rho (\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{y}) \leq \rho (\mathbf{x}) + \rho (\mathbf{y}))\)

  2. Absolute Homogeneity: \((\forall t \in F) (\forall \mathbf{x} \in V) (\rho (t\mathbf{x}) = | t | \rho (\mathbf{x}))\)

  3. Positive Definiteness: \((\forall \mathbf{x} \in V) (\rho (\mathbf{x}) \Leftrightarrow \mathbf{x}= \vec{0})\)

And derivable from these, we get that \(\)\((\forall \mathbf{x} \in V) (\rho (\mathbf{x}) \geq 0)\), which is called non-negativity.

Euclidean, \(l^1\), \(l^{\infty}\), and \(l^p\) Norms

Let \(p \geq 1\) be a real number. We define a \(l^p\) norm to be so that

\(\displaystyle \| \mathbf{x} \|_p := \left( \sum_{k = 1}^n | x_k |^p \right)^{\frac{1}{p}}\)

We say that the Euclidean norm on the \(n\)-dimensional Euclidean space \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is the same as the \(l^2\) norm so that \(\| \mathbf{x} \| := \| \mathbf{x} \|_2 = \sqrt{\mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{x}}\) where \(\cdot\) is the dot product.

When \(p \rightarrow \infty\), we get the \(l^{\infty}\) norm where \(\| \mathbf{x} \|_{\infty} := \underset{i}{\max} | x_i |\).

Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

\((\forall \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{R}^n)\)

\(\displaystyle | \mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{y} | \leq \| \mathbf{x} \| \cdot \| \mathbf{y} \|\)

with equality iff \(\mathbf{x}\) and \(\mathbf{y}\) are linearly dependant.

Triangle Inequality

Let \(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{R}^n\).

\(\displaystyle \| \mathbf{x}+\mathbf{y} \| \leq \| \mathbf{x} \| + \| \mathbf{y} \|\)

Angle between Vectors

Let \(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{R}^n\) both be non-zero. Cauchy-Schwarz says that

\(\displaystyle - 1 \leq \frac{\mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{y}}{\| \mathbf{x} \| \cdot \| \mathbf{y} \|} \leq 1\)

so that we see \(\left( \exists ! \theta \in \left[ 0, \frac{\pi}{2} \right] \right) (\mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{y}= \| \mathbf{x} \| \cdot \| \mathbf{y} \| \cos \theta)\). This \(\theta\) is the angle between the vectors \(\mathbf{x}\) and \(\mathbf{y}\).

Orthogonal Vectors

We say \(\mathbf{x}\) and \(\mathbf{y}\) are orthogonal iff \(\mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{y}= 0\).

Hyperplane

Let \(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^n\). The hyperplane \(P\) passing through \(\mathbf{a}\) with normal vector \(\mathbf{b}\) is defined to be

\(\displaystyle P := \{ \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n : (\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{b}= 0 \}\)
